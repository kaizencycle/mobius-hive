# Mobius HIVE — Proposal Flow v0.1

## Purpose

This document defines how civic proposals move through HIVE from idea to consequence.

A proposal in HIVE should not feel like a dry governance form.
It should feel like a public act that passes through:
- visibility
- response
- oath pressure
- legitimacy testing
- resolution
- memory

One-line canon:

> **A proposal is not just a suggestion. It is a structured test of whether the civilization wishes to move in a given direction.**

---

## Core thesis

Proposal flow is the mechanism by which HIVE turns:
- public intention
- Elder initiative
- institutional need
- civic pressure
- district change

into a process that can be seen, challenged, supported, and remembered.

A good proposal system must feel:
- public
- understandable
- costly enough to matter
- challengeable
- tied to visible world consequence

---

## Proposal sources

A proposal may originate from several places.

## 1. Elder proposal
A seated Elder proposes a decree, initiative, reform, or directional change.

### Nature
- high visibility
- high symbolic force
- legitimacy-sensitive

### Use cases
- district policy
- ritual changes
- seasonal directives
- major civic projects

---

## 2. Citizen proposal
A Citizen or citizen coalition proposes a public initiative.

### Nature
- bottom-up
- potentially slower to surface
- requires civic backing to matter

### Use cases
- repair effort
- market rule suggestion
- public festival request
- institutional accountability demand

---

## 3. Institutional proposal
A school, archive body, market guild, or agent-linked institution proposes something needed for system function.

### Nature
- role-specific
- domain-constrained
- often linked to stewardship rather than ideology

### Use cases
- transit repairs
- archive funding / preservation work
- school curriculum shifts
- marketplace structural changes

---

## 4. Crisis proposal
A proposal triggered by emergency, instability, or system pressure.

### Nature
- high urgency
- shorter response window
- more visible consequence

### Use cases
- emergency repair
- defensive routing
- quarantine or containment action
- temporary economic response

---

## Proposal lifecycle

Every proposal should move through a clear lifecycle.

## Stage 1 — Initiation
A proposer submits a proposal into the civic process.

### Requirements
- title
- purpose
- domain or affected district
- proposer identity or institutional source
- expected consequence
- visible cost or tradeoff

### Output
Proposal enters the public or institutional queue.

---

## Stage 2 — Public visibility
The proposal becomes visible to the relevant civic audience.

### Possible locations
- Citizen Plaza boards
- Castle of Elders announcement hall
- district-specific boards
- Festival Grounds during ceremonial periods
- White Room / Founding Core visibility for strategy review

### Rule
A proposal should be readable in plain, human terms.
It should not require expert parsing just to understand what is being asked.

---

## Stage 3 — Review and framing
The proposal is interpreted, clarified, and made legible.

### Possible review actors
- Elder sponsor or opposing Elder
- institutional stewards
- EVE-style constitutional synthesis
- Archive-linked memory comparison
- Citizen/public commentary layer

### Purpose
- clarify intent
- surface consequences
- expose tradeoffs
- connect it to existing world state

### Output
Proposal is now ready for oath participation or formal decision.

---

## Stage 4 — Oath participation
Citizens and relevant actors bind themselves to the proposal through oath mechanics.

### Available actions
- support oath
- opposition oath
- neutral oath
- service oath if implementation requires labor or stewardship

### Why this matters
This is where the proposal becomes real.
Without oath pressure, governance becomes weightless.

### Output
Proposal accumulates:
- support force
- opposition force
- civic participation record
- legitimacy impact
- service commitments where applicable

---

## Stage 5 — Resolution
The proposal resolves into an outcome.

### Possible outcomes
- passed
- failed
- contested
- deferred
- escalated

### Passed
Proposal gains implementation force.

### Failed
Proposal is rejected or does not gain enough support.

### Contested
Proposal is unstable, ambiguous, or enters challenge/review path.

### Deferred
Proposal remains open for later cycle or season.

### Escalated
Proposal moves upward into higher civic or Elder-level review.

---

## Stage 6 — World consequence
If the proposal passes, the world should visibly change.

### Examples
- district banners change
- transit routes open or close
- market conditions shift
- Archive records the new policy
- school curriculum expands
- festival format changes
- throne legitimacy shifts

### Rule
A passed proposal must produce visible consequence.
Otherwise governance will feel fake.

---

## Stage 7 — Memory and archive
All important proposals should leave a record.

### Archive-worthy proposal types
- throne-affecting proposals
- district-changing proposals
- major festival or seasonal proposals
- constitutional reforms
- crisis responses
- significant citizen-led initiatives

### Archive record should include
- who proposed it
- what was at stake
- who supported/opposed
- how it resolved
- what changed because of it

This is how HIVE remembers not just rulers, but decisions.

---

## Proposal classes

Different proposals should not all behave identically.

## 1. Local proposal
Affects one district or institution.

### Example
Repair the Reflection Garden’s lantern network.

### Traits
- smaller scope
- shorter resolution chain
- often more service-oath oriented

---

## 2. Civic proposal
Affects the general public life of the Dome.

### Example
Change market access rules during a season.

### Traits
- broader visibility
- more support/opposition weight
- stronger public memory implications

---

## 3. Constitutional proposal
Affects the deeper operating logic of the civilization.

### Example
Change the conditions under which Elder challenges can occur.

### Traits
- rare
- high scrutiny
- strong archive permanence
- potentially stronger Elder and institutional involvement

---

## 4. Crisis proposal
Affects immediate stability or continuity.

### Example
Temporary district lockdown or emergency transit reroute.

### Traits
- urgent
- shorter clock
- stronger visible consequence
- often heavier legitimacy implications

---

## Proposal and Elder relationship

Proposals are one of the main ways Elders express rule.
But they are also one of the main ways Elders can lose legitimacy.

### If an Elder’s proposal passes
- their symbolic authority strengthens
- public confidence may rise
- district effects may intensify

### If an Elder’s proposal fails
- legitimacy can weaken
- public opposition becomes visible
- challenge vulnerability may rise

### If an Elder abuses proposal power
- repeated failure, contested proposals, or unpopular overreach should destabilize the seat

This makes authority responsive rather than static.

---

## Proposal and Citizen relationship

Proposals are one of the main ways Citizens stop being passive observers.

A Citizen should feel that proposals allow them to:
- support the world they want
- resist the world they reject
- bind themselves to public work
- participate in shaping district life

This is essential to turning HIVE into a civic civilization rather than a royal spectacle.

---

## Proposal and service relationship

Not every proposal should end at voting.
Some should lead directly into work.

### Example
A transit repair proposal passes.
Then:
- service oaths open
- players bind themselves to rebuilding or stewardship
- the world visibly repairs over time

This makes proposal flow materially productive.

---

## Proposal UI logic

Proposal flow should be visible in-world and in overlays.

### World-facing surfaces
- public boards
- district signs
- Castle notices
- Festival displays
- Archive plaques after resolution

### Overlay-facing surfaces
- proposal detail view
- support / oppose / neutral oath actions
- service pledge interface
- timer and quorum indicators
- projected consequence summary

### Rule
The player should always know:
- what is being proposed
- who is asking for it
- what it will change
- what it will cost
- when it resolves

---

## Timing model

A proposal should have time gravity.

### Suggested time categories
#### Short-cycle proposal
- quick local issue
- short resolution window

#### Standard civic proposal
- several days or one cycle band
- visible discussion period

#### Seasonal proposal
- major public weight
- stronger ritual framing

#### Emergency proposal
- compressed clock
- elevated visibility

This prevents every proposal from feeling the same.

---

## Anti-failure rules

The proposal system must avoid becoming:
- bureaucratic sludge
- fake democracy theater
- a pure leaderboard of popularity
- endless low-stakes noise
- lore dressing with no consequence

### Guardrails
- proposals must have visible stakes
- proposal text must be readable
- oath participation must cost something meaningful
- passed proposals must change the world
- important proposals must enter memory

---

## MVP recommendation

For the first playable HIVE version, implement:
- one local proposal class
- one civic proposal class
- proposal detail panel
- support / oppose / neutral oath actions
- one visible district-level consequence
- one archive record output after resolution

This is enough to prove that civic decision-making in HIVE is real.
