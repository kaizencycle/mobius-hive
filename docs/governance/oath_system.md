# Mobius HIVE — Oath System v0.1

## Purpose

This document defines the **Oath System** as the binding mechanism through which Citizens, institutions, and public participants express civic support, opposition, alignment, and commitment inside HIVE.

The Oath System exists to prevent governance from becoming empty theater.

If governance matters, then public commitment must cost something real.

One-line canon:

> **An oath is where belief, cost, and consequence become visible at once.**

---

## Core thesis

An oath in HIVE is not just dialogue flavor.
It is a meaningful civic action.

Through oaths, players can:
- support an Elder
- oppose an Elder
- support a proposal
- refuse a proposal
- commit to a festival
- bind themselves to a public cause
- strengthen or weaken legitimacy

An oath should feel like:
- ritualized governance
- public intent
- costly commitment
- visible consequence

---

## Why the oath system exists

The Oath System solves several civilizational problems at once.

### 1. It makes governance legible
Players can see who is supporting what.

### 2. It makes governance costly
Players must commit value, time, or status to public positions.

### 3. It makes legitimacy dynamic
Authority rises or weakens based on actual civic support.

### 4. It makes ritual meaningful
The civilization feels ceremonial without becoming fake.

### 5. It creates public memory
Important oaths can become part of the Archive.

---

## Oath types

## 1. Support Oath
Used when a Citizen wishes to strengthen a person, institution, proposal, or civic direction.

### Example uses
- support an Elder
- support a decree
- support a district initiative
- support a festival outcome

### Typical effect
- increases legitimacy or momentum
- burns or locks a smaller amount than opposition in most cases
- records visible support

---

## 2. Opposition Oath
Used when a Citizen wishes to resist, weaken, or challenge a person, institution, or decree.

### Example uses
- oppose an Elder
- oppose a proposal
- signal public dissatisfaction
- accelerate throne instability

### Typical effect
- weakens legitimacy faster
- costs more than support in most cases
- carries greater weight and greater sacrifice

This matters because opposition should not be free outrage.

---

## 3. Neutral Oath
Used when a Citizen wishes to stay aligned with the civic process but not strongly empower either side.

### Example uses
- preserve optionality
- acknowledge importance of an issue without escalation
- support process over faction

### Typical effect
- lower impact
- lower cost
- contributes to quorum or presence without decisive force

---

## 4. Service Oath
Used when a Citizen binds themselves to a public task, repair project, institution, or civic need.

### Example uses
- district repair
- archive stewardship
- festival service
- school or mentorship duty
- public-goods work

### Typical effect
- grants role-linked contribution pathways
- can create visible public service identity
- may yield integrity or civic rewards when fulfilled

---

## 5. Festival Oath
Used during formal festival periods, elections, succession, seasonal transitions, or public rites.

### Example uses
- Festival of Echoes
- Elder support / oppose rituals
- seasonal legitimacy ceremonies
- public renewal events

### Typical effect
- strongest ceremonial visibility
- often includes burn + lock components
- most likely to enter public memory and archive

---

## Oath mechanics

An oath should usually include some combination of these elements.

## 1. Public declaration
The player visibly chooses a side, stance, or commitment.

## 2. Cost
The player pays something real.
Examples:
- token burn
- temporary lock
- public reputation exposure
- limited civic bandwidth

## 3. Duration
Some oaths should remain active for a period of time.

## 4. Consequence
The oath changes a real system:
- legitimacy
- proposal strength
- district morale
- institutional standing
- festival outcome

## 5. Memory trace
Some oaths should be archived as part of the civilization’s history.

---

## Cost model

Oaths should not all cost the same.

### Suggested cost hierarchy
#### Support Oath
- moderate cost
- moderate civic effect

#### Opposition Oath
- higher cost
- higher destabilizing effect

#### Neutral Oath
- low cost
- low direct force

#### Service Oath
- variable cost
- paid more through time, action, or role commitment

#### Festival Oath
- medium to high cost
- strongest ceremonial visibility and public force

---

## Why opposition should cost more

This is important.

If opposition is free, governance collapses into noise.
If support is free, governance collapses into fake applause.

So HIVE should treat opposition as a serious act:
- it should carry higher sacrifice
- it should be stronger when used
- it should not be spammed casually

This preserves both conflict and seriousness.

---

## Oath and Elder legitimacy

The Oath System is one of the main forces governing Elder stability.

### Support Oath effect on Elder
- increases legitimacy
- stabilizes throne
- improves decree confidence
- may reinforce district effects

### Opposition Oath effect on Elder
- weakens legitimacy
- increases challenge vulnerability
- may reduce district confidence
- can push a throne toward fragile or broken state

### Neutral Oath effect on Elder
- contributes presence but not strongly toward stabilization or destabilization

This is the bridge between citizen will and elite office.

---

## Oath and proposals

Proposals should not simply be yes/no votes.
Oaths make proposal participation more embodied.

### Proposal oath examples
- support with moderate stake
- oppose with higher stake
- neutral acknowledgement
- service oath to implement if passed

This creates richer governance than flat button presses.

---

## Oath and festivals

Festivals should be the most visible oath environments in HIVE.

### Why
Festivals are where:
- public emotion
- governance
- economy
- memory
- ritual

all overlap.

That makes them the perfect place for oath intensity.

### Festival oath examples
- support an Elder publicly
- oppose a decree during Festival of Echoes
- bind to seasonal reconstruction effort
- participate in a renewal rite

Festival oaths should feel unforgettable.

---

## Oath and service identity

Service Oaths allow Citizens to bind themselves to something larger than faction.

Examples:
- protect the Archive for one cycle
- repair the Market Quarter after crisis
- support the School during a festival season
- steward a transit route or shrine

This is important because not every civic commitment should be political conflict.
Some oaths should be about stewardship.

---

## Public visibility rules

Oaths should not all be equally visible.

### Publicly visible by default
- Elder support / opposition
- proposal-aligned major oaths
- festival oaths
- major service oaths

### Private or semi-private by default
- preparatory reflections before oath
- some personal service vows
- low-stakes neutral alignments

### Rule
Oaths should feel public enough to matter, but not so exposed that every act becomes performative noise.

---

## Archive rules

Some oaths deserve memory.

### Archive-worthy oaths
- throne-changing oaths
- major festival oaths
- public service oaths that altered district state
- foundational or historic proposal oaths

### Why
This ensures the civilization remembers not only rulers and outcomes, but also the public commitments that shaped them.

---

## Oath lifecycle

Every oath should have a lifecycle.

### 1. Intent
The player chooses to make an oath.

### 2. Declaration
The oath is spoken, confirmed, or ritualized.

### 3. Cost application
The system burns, locks, or binds relevant value.

### 4. Active state
The oath has ongoing civic force.

### 5. Resolution
The oath either:
- succeeds
- expires
- is fulfilled
- is broken
- is archived

### 6. Memory
Important oaths may persist as part of public record.

---

## Broken oaths

Broken oaths should matter.

### Possible causes
- service vow abandoned
- support oath withdrawn under instability
- institutional duty failed
- ritual commitment not honored

### Effects
- trust or integrity penalty
- visible public disappointment where appropriate
- archive mark for major cases
- reduced future oath credibility

This makes commitment meaningful.

---

## World-facing effects

The Oath System should visibly alter the world.

### Examples
- oath pools fill in Festival Grounds
- throne banners brighten or dim
- district statues or flames change state
- public boards show strong civic alignment shifts
- service-oath areas gain repair scaffolds, lanterns, or visible stewardship markers

This keeps oaths embodied rather than abstract.

---

## Scout vs Citizen relationship to oaths

### Scout
Should usually observe oath systems before deeply participating in them.
A Scout may witness, learn, or make a very small first commitment.

### Citizen
Can meaningfully support, oppose, bind, and enter service or festival oath structures.

This preserves the importance of the Scout → Citizen transition.

---

## Anti-failure rules

The Oath System must avoid becoming:
- fake ceremonial flavor
- free outrage mechanics
- loyalty farming
- shallow popularity theater
- meaningless stake spam

### Guardrails
- every major oath should have real cost
- every major oath should have visible consequence
- service oaths must matter, not just factional oaths
- archive memory should preserve the most meaningful public commitments

---

## MVP recommendation

For the first playable HIVE version, implement:
- support oath
- opposition oath
- one simple service oath
- visible oath cost
- visible legitimacy impact
- one archive-worthy oath event type

This is enough to make governance feel bound and public without overbuilding the full ritual system.
